Frequently Asked Questions

WHAT YOU NEED TO KNOW ABOUT LIVE STREAMING

Answers to the most common questions our clients ask before planning a live stream or video production.

Scroll

How fast does my internet connection have to be to stream?

For HD live streams, our crew recommends that your venue has at least 100 Mbps upload and download speed in order to maintain a clear and consistent internet connection. 100 Mbps will maintain HD quality for an entire stream, while 50 Mbps is a standard quality stream and 25 Mbps is low quality. Upload speed matters more than download speed. Free testing tools like speedtest.net help verify connections.

What is the difference between a hybrid live stream and a fully virtual event?

Hybrid live streams are what most people typically think of when they think of a live streamed event. Meaning, a live event (potentially with an in-person audience) like a presentation, concert, or discussion panel that is also streamed online. Fully virtual events happen completely online with speakers often in different locations, each offering distinct advantages and disadvantages.

How much does it cost to live stream?

Pricing varies by complexity. Simple live streams with one camera for half a day can start as low as $1,500. Larger streams that have multiple locations or require multiple cameras can range anywhere from $3,500 to $15,000. An element that often raises the cost of streaming is the amount of pre and post production assets required.

What can I do to make sure my stream looks the best it can look?

Internet speed, quality cameras, and a reliable streaming platform are all important pieces of making sure your live stream goes well. But professional services address additional elements. Making sure your speakers are properly lit and audio levels are consistent are equally important to hosting a professional live stream.

The audio for my live stream is complicated. What is the best way to make sure viewers can hear everything clearly?

Making sure to have an audio professional for your venue is the best way to make sure all of your speakers are audible. Audio engineers are even more important when your event includes videos or other multimedia elements that need to be mixed into the stream audio.

How long does it take to plan a live stream?

Timeline depends on complexity. If your stream is simple, Transcendent's crew could be ready to go live within 48 hours. Other more complicated streams that include speakers from around the world or feature lots of multimedia elements can take a few weeks to fully plan.

Can I use WiFi to broadcast my live stream?

Technically it is possible to stream over WiFi, but Transcendent strongly advises against using wireless internet. WiFi's unreliability risks connection loss or quality degradation when other users access the same network. We always recommend a wired ethernet connection for the most stable broadcast.

How do I keep viewers engaged?

The best way to keep the viewer's attention is by being creative. Include video assets, branded transition slides, and interactive tools like polling features to maintain audience attention. Looking at other company's live events can help inspire you with new elements to add to your live stream.

How many cameras do we need to capture my event?

Simple single-speaker events work with one camera (or zero for fully virtual events). However, if your event is complicated or is a high-value event for your company, we recommend a minimum of 2 cameras. Complex multi-location events need additional cameras for comprehensive coverage.

Planning Guide

Steps to Planning a Professional Live Stream

1
Set Goals & Choose Location
Set clear goals for your live stream, then choose a location and time for your event. Consider time zones for speakers and remote audience members early in the planning process.
2
Choose Crew & Platform
Decide on your live stream crew and choose what platform you want to stream on. Transcendent combines YouTube, Vimeo, and Zoom with vMix for branded, professional broadcasts.
3
Rehearse & Go Live
Go over the stream itinerary, conduct a walkthrough and tech check of the venue, then go live with confidence. After the stream, assess results and plan post-production deliverables.
